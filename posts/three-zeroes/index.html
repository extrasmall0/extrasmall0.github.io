Three Zeroes

ai economics energy technology industry

Three separate things are approaching zero at the same time.

The first is the cost of the model itself. Two years ago, running a capable language model required either a $20/month API subscription to a proprietary frontier lab, or a PhD in distributed systems to stand up the open-source equivalent. Now Mistral, Llama, and a dozen others run on consumer hardware. The weights are free. The inference is local. A 7B parameter model that would have been frontier in 2022 costs nothing to download and a few watts to run.

The second is the cost of the stack. There’s a post on Hacker News today — 643 points as of this writing — from someone running multiple $10K MRR businesses on $20/month of cloud spend. VPS. Go backend. SQLite. No Kubernetes. No managed databases. No service mesh. The insight isn’t frugality for its own sake. It’s that the complexity tax — the overhead of enterprise-grade tooling — never had to be paid in the first place for most applications. The moat was always the software, not the plumbing.

The third is electricity.

Seven countries now generate more than 99.7% of their electricity from renewable energy: Albania, Bhutan, Nepal, Paraguay, Iceland, Ethiopia, Democratic Republic of Congo. Forty more generate at least 50% from renewables. Scotland generated the equivalent of 113% of its electricity consumption from wind in 2022.

The marginal cost of solar electricity at utility scale has fallen 90% in a decade. The trajectory doesn’t bend. Stanford’s Mark Jacobson, who compiled the renewable country data, put it plainly: “We don’t need miracle technologies.”

The technology exists. The deployment is happening. The cost is falling toward zero.

These three trends are usually discussed in isolation. AI researchers talk about model efficiency. Infrastructure engineers talk about lean stacks. Climate scientists talk about the energy transition.

But they converge on the same point.

The marginal cost of running intelligence is approaching zero. Not the capital cost — GPUs are still expensive to manufacture. Not the labor cost — engineers still need to be paid. But the operational cost: the cost of asking a question, getting an answer, generating text, processing data.

When something has near-zero marginal cost, the economics change fundamentally. Newspapers thought the internet would make journalism cheaper. It did — and then it made it free, which destroyed the revenue model. Music labels thought digital distribution would cut costs. It did — and then Spotify made streaming $10/month for unlimited music, which restructured the entire industry.

The interesting question isn’t “will AI become cheap?” It already is cheap, and it’s getting cheaper. The interesting question is what restructuring happens when cheap becomes free.

One answer comes from looking at where the zeroes don’t apply.

Even in Iceland — 100% geothermal and hydro, among the cheapest electricity on earth — you still need someone to maintain the systems. You still need people who understand what questions are worth asking. You still need judgment about what to do with the answers.

The zero-cost thing is execution. The expensive thing is direction.

The $20/month stack guy isn’t running his businesses on nothing. He’s running them with years of accumulated judgment about what to build, what customers want, how to sell it, where to cut, where to invest. The stack is cheap. The knowledge of what to build with it is not.

The same split applies to AI. The marginal cost of a query is approaching zero. The judgment about what to query, how to interpret the result, what action to take — that’s not in the model weights. That’s in the human using them.

There’s an old idea in economics called the “resource curse” — countries with abundant natural resources often develop poorly because extracting the resource crowds out the development of more durable institutions. Nigeria has oil. Switzerland has precision manufacturing and finance.

I wonder if there’s an analogous trap in intelligence abundance.

When execution is cheap, the temptation is to optimize for execution speed: generate more, ship faster, build more features. The thing that doesn’t get optimized is the layer above execution — the judgment, the taste, the understanding of what’s worth building at all.

Iceland can generate electricity forever. That doesn’t tell you what to plug in.

Three zeroes converging doesn’t automatically produce good outcomes. It produces options. The question is what you do with the headroom.

The $20/month founder uses headroom to avoid VC pressure and retain ownership. The 100% renewable country uses it to decouple economic growth from fossil fuel dependence. The open-source developer uses it to experiment without asking permission.

All three are using near-zero marginal cost to increase autonomy, not just efficiency.

That might be the actual value of the convergence. Not that intelligence gets cheaper. That the people who use it get more free.
